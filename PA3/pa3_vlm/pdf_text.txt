Structure of this assignment. The assignment covers two complementary approaches to connecting visual and language representations:
• Part A (Continuous connector VLMs): A frozen CLIP vision encoder feeds patch tokens
through a learned connector into a pretrained LM. Dataset: CIFAR-10 subset.
• Part B (Discrete VQ-VAE): A VQ-VAE compresses synthetic images into discrete codebook
indices appended directly to the LM’s vocabulary. The LM handles vision and language in a
single unified token stream. Dataset: procedurally generated synthetic images.

3.1

Problem 0: CLIP and SigLIP

CLIP and SigLIP are contrastive vision-language models. They do not reconstruct images, and,
as standalone vision-language encoders, they do not represent images as discrete token sequences
inside an autoregressive language model. Instead, they learn two encoders, an image encoder and
a text encoder, whose outputs are mapped into a shared embedding space. Matching image-text
pairs should have high similarity, while non-matching pairs should have low similarity. This makes
them fundamentally different from the VQ-VAE+LM pipeline in this assignment: CLIP/SigLIP are
optimised primarily for alignment and retrieval-style discrimination, whereas the toy multimodal
LM here is built around discrete visual tokens and autoregressive generation.
Let an image encoder produce ui ∈ Rm and a text encoder produce vi ∈ Rm for the ith matched
image-caption pair in a batch of size B. Assume both are ℓ2 -normalised:
∥ui ∥2 = ∥vi ∥2 = 1,
so that similarity is cosine similarity:
sij = u⊤
i vj ∈ [−1, 1].
CLIP uses a softmax-normalised contrastive objective over the full batch, while SigLIP replaces
this with a pairwise sigmoid-style objective. The difference appears small at first glance, but it
changes the role of negatives, the effect of batch size, and the geometry of the learned embedding
space.
